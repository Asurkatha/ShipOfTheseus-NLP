\subsection{Controlled Experiments: The 5.8$\times$ Sensitivity Gap (RQ1)}

Using Dipper Low vs.\ High as a controlled comparison (identical architecture, different intensity), content features (NER Recall delta: 0.610) are \textbf{5.8$\times$ more sensitive} to paraphrasing intensity than stylistic features (POS cosine delta: 0.104). Figure~\ref{fig:dipper_intensity} visualizes this asymmetry. The Pegasus coverage experiment (Slight: 25\% vs.\ Full: 100\% of sentences) confirms that paraphrasers primarily \emph{shed} entities (Recall drops 0.329) rather than introducing novel ones (Precision drops only 0.122).

\subsection{Authorship Attribution Decay (RQ2)}

We train three classifiers---Logistic Regression, SVM (RBF), and Random Forest---on the 23-dimensional feature vector extracted from $T_0$ texts, with binary source labels (Human vs.\ LLM). We then evaluate each classifier on the same articles at $T_1$, $T_2$, and $T_3$ to measure how quickly authorial identity becomes unrecoverable. All classifiers use balanced class weighting and standardized features.

Figure~\ref{fig:attribution_decay} shows the F1 decay curves across six paraphrasers and three classifiers. For Logistic Regression---the most interpretable model---macro F1 drops below 0.5 (random baseline for balanced binary classification) at $T_1$ for Dipper (High), confirming that a single aggressive paraphrase iteration is sufficient to erase authorial identity from stylometric features. Pegasus (Slight) retains the most signal, with F1 remaining above 0.5 through $T_3$ for ensemble methods.

\subsection{Paraphraser Identification (RQ3)}

We construct 24-dimensional delta feature vectors ($T_1 - T_0$ for POS proportions, dependency depth, and text statistics, plus NER Jaccard/Recall/Precision) and train a 6-class classifier to identify which paraphraser produced a given $T_1$ text. PaLM2 is excluded due to insufficient shared articles (only 37 common (key, source) pairs across all 7 paraphrasers; excluding PaLM2 yields 1,546 common pairs). We use 80/20 stratified train-test splits.

SVM (RBF) achieves the highest macro F1 of \textbf{0.755} at 6-class paraphraser identification, confirming that each paraphraser leaves a distinct forensic fingerprint in the delta feature space. Figure~\ref{fig:confusion} shows the confusion matrix: Dipper (High) is the most identifiable (93\% correct classification) due to its extreme transformation signature, while Dipper (Low) is most confusable with Dipper Default (23\% misclassified).

Random Forest feature importance (Figure~\ref{fig:importance}) reveals that the most discriminative features are NER Jaccard (entity preservation), word count ratio (text compression), and NER Precision (novel entity introduction rate). \subsection{Domain-Specific Analysis}

Decay patterns vary by domain (Figure~\ref{fig:domain_heatmap}). Entity-rich domains like XSum (18.7 entities/doc) show better NER retention (Recall 0.461) than low-entity domains like Yelp (5.9 entities, Recall 0.362). CMV (arguments) has the most resilient POS structure (cosine 0.976), while ELI5 attribution collapses nearest to chance (F1 = 0.542 at $T_1$). Formal, structured domains preserve more stylometric signal than casual text.
