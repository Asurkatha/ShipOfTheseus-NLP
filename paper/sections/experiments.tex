\section{Experimentation and Results}

Our initial experiments establish baseline measurements of how text changes across paraphrase iterations. We focus on two analyses: (1) lexical vs.\ semantic similarity decay, and (2) exploratory analysis of stylistic feature shifts.

\subsection{Similarity Baselines}

We computed three similarity metrics between each original text $T_0$ and its paraphrased versions $T_1$, $T_2$, $T_3$ across all seven paraphraser variants:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BLEU} \cite{papineni2002bleu}: Sentence-level smoothed n-gram precision. A lexical metric measuring surface word overlap.
    \item \textbf{ROUGE-L} \cite{lin2004rouge}: Longest common subsequence F1 score. Also lexical, capturing sequential word preservation.
    \item \textbf{BERTScore} \cite{zhang2020bertscore}: Contextual embedding similarity using DeBERTa-xlarge-MNLI. A semantic metric capturing meaning preservation beyond surface words.
\end{itemize}

We emphasize that BLEU and ROUGE are purely lexical metrics. They measure how many words survive paraphrasing, not whether meaning is preserved. BERTScore addresses this gap by comparing texts in embedding space.

\textbf{Key finding:} Across all paraphrasers, BERTScore remains between 0.80--0.98, while BLEU drops as low as 0.002. This confirms our core hypothesis: semantic meaning (the ``hull'') is preserved while lexical surface features (the ``planks'') are systematically replaced.

The sharpest decay occurs at $T_1$. For ChatGPT, BLEU drops 81\% from $T_0$ to $T_1$ but only an additional 5\% from $T_1$ to $T_3$. This suggests the first paraphrase iteration causes the most damage to authorial identity, with diminishing changes thereafter.

\subsection{Exploratory Analysis}

Beyond similarity metrics, we analyzed how text characteristics shift across tiers.

\textbf{Text shortening:} Average word count decreases monotonically from 279 ($T_0$) to 221 ($T_1$) to 198 ($T_2$) to 184 ($T_3$). Figure~\ref{fig:length_ratio} shows the $T_3$/$T_0$ word count ratio for each dataset--paraphraser combination. Dipper (High) and PaLM2 produce the most aggressive compression (ratios as low as 0.27 and 0.34), while Pegasus (Slight) and Dipper (Low) preserve length best (0.82--0.90). The pattern is consistent across all seven datasets, though sci\_gen is most affected by Dipper (High) (ratio 0.27).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/eda/t3_t0_length_ratio_heatmap.png}
\Description{Heatmap of word count ratios from T3 to T0 across seven datasets and seven paraphrasers.}
\caption{$T_3$/$T_0$ word count ratio by dataset and paraphraser. Green indicates similar length to the original; red indicates significant compression. Dipper (High) and PaLM2 consistently compress text across all datasets, while Pegasus (Slight) preserves length.}
\label{fig:length_ratio}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Punctuation erosion:} Figure~\ref{fig:punctuation} shows that expressive punctuation marks decay monotonically across tiers. Exclamation marks decrease by approximately 63\% from $T_0$ to $T_3$, semicolons by 36\%, and question marks by 35\%. Commas are the most resilient, declining only 18\%. This monotonic decay of stylistic markers, while semantic content is preserved (as shown by BERTScore), directly supports the Ship of Theseus analogy: the structural ``planks'' of writing style are being replaced one by one.

\end{figure}

\textbf{Vocabulary shift:} Examining the top 20 words in Yelp reviews before and after three ChatGPT paraphrase iterations reveals a shift from informal, personal language (``you'', ``very'', ``great'', ``really'') to formal, generic register (``however'', ``despite'', ``experience'', ``recommend''). This is direct evidence of what we term \emph{style laundering}: personal voice is systematically replaced by a generic LLM register while topical content (``food'', ``service'', ``place'') persists.

\subsection{Dipper Intensity: The 5.8$\times$ Sensitivity Gap (RQ1)}

Using Dipper Low vs.\ High as a controlled experiment with identical architecture but different intensity settings, we isolate the effect of aggressiveness on style vs.\ content:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{POS cosine delta:} $0.984 - 0.878 = 0.104$ (style drifts modestly)
    \item \textbf{NER Recall delta:} $0.648 - 0.038 = 0.610$ (content collapses)
\end{itemize}

Content features are \textbf{5.8$\times$ more sensitive} to paraphrasing intensity than stylistic features. Figure~\ref{fig:dipper_intensity} visualizes this asymmetry.

\subsection{Pegasus Controlled Experiment: Dropping vs.\ Novel Introduction}

Pegasus (Slight) paraphrases 25\% of sentences; Pegasus (Full) paraphrases 100\%. Comparing their NER metrics isolates the effect of coverage:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recall drops} 0.329 (from 0.887 to 0.557)---broader coverage causes more entity \emph{dropping}.
    \item \textbf{Precision drops} only 0.122 (from 0.967 to 0.846)---novel entity introduction increases modestly.
\end{itemize}

This pattern holds consistently across all seven datasets. The Recall gap (entity dropping) ranges from 0.298 (XSum) to 0.393 (SCI\_GEN), while the Precision gap stays narrow (0.103--0.134). Paraphrasers primarily \emph{shed} entities by rephrasing around them; novel entity introduction remains modest even at full coverage.

\subsection{Human vs.\ LLM Source Author}

Splitting the corpus by whether $T_0$ was authored by a human or an LLM reveals a subtle but consistent pattern: human-authored texts show \emph{slightly less} POS drift than LLM-generated texts (ChatGPT: 0.974 vs.\ 0.967; Dipper High: 0.892 vs.\ 0.880). The effect is small (delta $\approx$ 0.007) but consistent across both paraphrasers and most datasets.

We hypothesize that the six LLM source authors (OpenAI, PaLM, LLaMA, GLM, BLOOM, GPT-NeoX) produce syntactically heterogeneous $T_0$ texts, each with distinct POS biases. When a single paraphraser rewrites these diverse inputs, it normalizes them toward its own syntactic template, producing larger POS shifts. Human writing, by contrast, follows more conventional syntactic patterns that resist restructuring.

This demonstrates that fingerprints are \emph{multi-dimensional}---a single metric cannot distinguish all paraphrasers, but the combination of style and content signals creates separable forensic signatures.

\textbf{Key findings from multi-tier analysis:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{POS cosine degrades gradually.} Even at $T_3$, the most aggressive paraphraser (Dipper High) retains 0.843 cosine similarity. The grammatical skeleton erodes slowly and proportionally across iterations.
    \item \textbf{NER Recall continues to drop steeply.} For ChatGPT, entity retention drops from 64\% ($T_1$) to 47\% ($T_3$)---a 17-point decline across two additional iterations. For Dipper (High), entities are virtually eliminated by $T_2$ (1.8\%) and remain near zero at $T_3$ (1.2\%).
    \item \textbf{The layered erosion pattern holds at all depths.} At every tier, the ordering is consistent: BERTScore $>$ POS cosine $>$ NER Recall $>$ ROUGE-L $>$ BLEU. No feature type ``catches up'' to another, confirming that the erosion rates are intrinsic to the feature type, not an artifact of the first iteration.
\end{enumerate}

We also measured dependency tree depth, which shows modest compression for Pegasus (Full) (mean depth decreasing from 5.40 to 4.17 across $T_0$ to $T_3$) but minimal change for other paraphrasers, suggesting that syntactic complexity is largely preserved even when surface features are replaced.
